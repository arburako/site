About the project

ParkPlease launched it’s service in May 2012 for San Francisco’s Outside Lands Music Festival, making city and regional headlines. Since then, ParkPlease has connected thousands of parking space owners to drivers looking for spaces and has successfully facilitated parking for numerous events including America’s Cup, Fleet Week, and the Hardly Strictly Music Festival. As a co-founder, I worked with a core team of 3 team to create, launch, and manage ParkPlease from June of 2012 through August of 2013.

My Role

I managed the product roadmap, led the design process, oversaw the development and did my best to keep everyone running in the same direction. I also created the website flows, interactions and layouts from the ground up, and became responsible for the visual design and user operations after launch.

Stress Free Parking

The overall goal of ParkPlease is to destress parking

Designing a site that works in multiple languages brings a unique set of challenges. If you are reading this, you probably know that English is written from left to right, as are all Germanic languages and in fact most languages. However some of the languages the site was translated to, like Farsi, are written and read from right to left. This means that all layouts needed to work mirrored, this includes all background images used for text and all UI elements.

Translating also means that you need to factor in different lengths of sentences depending on the language, making precise layouts and word placements next to impossible. German is for instance notoriously long: hovercraft (a word that was for some reason never used on the Google+ site) in german is Luftkissenfahrzeug. When large chunks of copy are translated, the compounded effect of this can wreak havoc on any layout.

Stress Free Parking

The first question to answer was - what problem will the product solve and how? When making products from scratch, it can be tempting to spend a lot of up-front time trying to formulate a flawless strategy. The team had done some valuable analysis of the market size and industry potential. But what they really needed was get to a simple, actionable statement about what problem we were going to solve for the user as soon as possible, so that the design process can proceed. As a means to that end, I led the team through a brainstorm session about to refine the problem that our product would solve.

  • Transparency

    Both buyers and sellers want a clear understanding of pricing with the ability to easily compare rates and features across listings in the same area

  • Ease of Use

    Managing and/or renting a spot should be entirely straightforward, simple and even fun. Seller or buyer, using our site should never feel like a burden.

  • Community

    Users need to know the transaction takes place within a framework of credibility where there is a standard of behavior and consequences for misbehavior.
