Limos. For Everyone.

On Demand

About the Project

Limos.com is an online and mobile marketplace for limousine and chauffeured cars with a robust car service reservation system and network of over 2,000 Operators. The company had initially focused on the corporate market, offering only “scheduled” service where a reservation must be booked at least an hour in advance. In June 2011, the company saw potential for launching a bigger service that would allow anyone to request and receive car service within the same hour. This new product offering was called the On Demand app. The whole endeavor included a customer facing iPhone app, an Operator facing Android app, an API integration with a partnering service, as well as multiple site and admin features to support the service. My team was tasked with creating the customer experience and supporting technology from start to finish.

My Role

As Product Manager, I was responsible for coordinating all the moving parts of for this brand new product offering (mentioned above). I was also the user experience designer for the app creating all flows and wireframes across numerous platforms (iPhone, android, admin web, consumer web). Last, I managed the development cycle for each component, translating my designs into detailed development requirements, working closely with engineers to resolve technical hurdles and systematically communicating with business stakeholders.

Limos for everyone

At the start of the project, Limos.com had an existing iPhone app that handling scheduling reservations - their core service. Our task was to create a new iPhone app that integrated the existing flow for scheduling reservations and our newly created On Demand functionality into one Limos.com app. The main emphasis for the project was on the On Demand side of the app as the company had recently spent time and resources optimizing their existing scheduled flow. The overarching goal for the On Demand iPhone app was to create a simple, easy,

straightforward solution for customers looking to book and receive car service within the hour and seamlessly integrate that product offering with our current Scheduled Reservations Product. Furthermore, a main priority of the project was time to market. Services like Uber and TaxiMagic were growing fast and we wanted to get in the game. However, our plan was to roll out the On Demand functionality market by market, so the app had to seamlessly support the following use cases.

Scheduled

The existing technology was built for the user who was planning ahead for a ride sometime in the future - for instance, “I want to schedule my transportation to the airport tomorrow.”

Real Time

The primary use case that On Demand would cater to was the “pick me up right here, right now” scenario. This means that the user is looking for the first available ride from their current location. The design priorities for this flow were to minimize user inputs and required clicks to order a ride to as few as possible.

Near Term

We believed that users would greatly benefit from being able to select 15, 20, 30 minutes - the “near-term” customer. Instead of being immediately paired with the nearest driver, the user could, for instance, request a car for when they arrive home in 30 minutes.

Initial Strategy

Our initial strategy for On Demand development was to focus all efforts on the new On Demand side of the app and leave the existing Scheduled Reservations side as untouched as possible. This meant we would focus on solving for the “real time” and “near term” use cases - the On Demand use cases - as completely separate from the “scheduled” use case, which already had an existing app. Prior to launch we would skin the Scheduled app look and feel to be consistent with the new app. Though it wouldn’t be perfect, it would be live. We could learn and iterate from there.


Based on this strategy, the initial designs featured equal entry to On Demand and Scheduled Reservations with a landing screen that asked the user to choose when they wanted to ride. From there, the user was funneled down either the new On Demand flow or the existing reservations flow.

Using a splash screen to bifurcate the On Demand flow from the Scheduled flow had the following benefits:

  • It allowed us to isolate out the “near term” and “real time” use cases - the On Demand use cases - from the “scheduled” use cases, seemingly simplifying the overall body of work to address as well as freeing the On Demand design and development from the existing constraints and limitations of the “scheduled” use case.

  • It balanced the entry to the On Demand product with the entry to the Schedule product. In markets where On Demand was not yet live, the app would simply start on the existing “scheduled” homepage.

  • It was the best way to leverage the entirety of the existing functionality we had already built in the “scheduled” app, ensuring the On Demand development had minimal effect on the existing functionality and thus minimizing potential “re-work” of the existing app.

We continued down this path for some time, focusing only on the On Demand side of the app and figuring out the user experience problems that were unique to that set of functionality in isolation from the rest of the app.

Developing the On Demand Functionality

Leaving the Scheduled functionality aside, we were free to focus on the user experience problems that were unique to the On Demand flow. In an increasingly crowded market, we didn’t want to release another “me-too” product and so, as a first step, we thought carefully about what our product differentiators would be and thought carefully about how to design and develop for these new product principles.

Finding Rides

The primary use case that On Demand would cater to was the “pick me up right here, right now” scenario. In order to best serve that user, we wanted the app to display the first available pick up time and nearby drivers based on the user’s current location upon screen load. This would mean displaying cars on the map and the first available ride time at screen load, prior to any user inputs - a flow for which there was no prior system precedence.

A secondary use cases came from one of our main product differentiators with our On Demand app. There were already a few services that providing rides for the “right-here-right-now” or “real-time” customer. But we believed that users would greatly benefit from being able to select 15, 20, 30 minutes - the “near-term” customer. Instead of being immediately paired with the nearest driver, the user could, for instance, request a car for when they arrive home in 30 minutes. We believed this was a powerful product differentiator but it also further complicated the already complex interaction of the first screen. For the “near term” user, the available drivers displayed on screen load (based on current time and location) was at best irrelevant and at worst, a bait and switch.

We decided that whenever the customer moved the map, the time options for the new location would change and the customer would be notified via an slot-machine-like animation on the time and location form fields. This animation would serve as subtle indication to the user that the search results had been updated and an intuitive reminder that changing the location will change available times.

Rethinking Rates

Another differentiator of the On Demand app was our pricing system. Most services at the time had a formula based rate system, much like taxis. However, we wanted our provide our users with the comfort of 0 variability in pricing - meaning that going from point A to point B would be the same rate every time regardless of situational influences on time and distance. This means the user can sit back and relax during the ride, without having to worry about what route the driver is taking or how much traffic there is at the time.

We wanted to communicate this benefit to the user in an elegant way and also set expectations up front for how much a ride would cost. In order to calculate any given rate, however, the system needed a pickup and drop off location. But given given that the primary design principle for the On Demand users was fewest possible clicks, we wanted to avoid asking the user to enter a destination. So how could we communicate pricing up front without needlessly encumbering the ordering process?

To solve this problem, we created “the rate card”, that featured the flat rates to nearby airports (the most common type of ride) and an interactive, fun way for the user to get a feel for the pricing structure that re-used the development logic from end ride pricing.

Turning to Scheduled

When we felt we had a good handle on the On Demand requirements, we turned to the Scheduled app. The plan had always been to make minor updates to the scheduled side so that it was consistent with the On Demand experience. After an initial analysis of the scheduled side, I began to think it was based on some system assumptions that were not translating into a truly useable user experience.


I presented a detailed analysis of our current flow including suggestions for modifications on existing screens to improve the user experience. I then presented three options for how we could proceed on the scheduled app ranging from barely alter the flow to completely re-do the flow as if we had no time-to-market requirement.

Analysis of current flow

VERSION 1: least departure from existing flow, kept flow mostly the same, OD address input replaces current address input, update to controls on detail screen, search results re-design to be more user center, removed multiple reservation confirmation screens

VERSION 2: somewhat of a departure from existing flow, removed back and forth from input screen, user is just funneled forward; update to controls; search results re-design to be more user centric; removed multiple reservation confirmation screens

VERSION 3: largest departure from existing flow; removed 4 tile screen, ride type can be inferred, not selected; update to controls; srp redesign; removed multiple conf screens.

Rethinking the Strategy

After discussing as team what the app might look like if we left time-to-market out of the equation, we realized that our original strategy had been wrong. We decided to re-think the app as one flow taking the emphasis off leveraging existing functionality and moving towards thinking like the user, not the system.


I presented three flows to the team ranging from slight departure from the existing On Demand spec and scheduled reservations live app to largest departure. As a team, we decided to go with the flow that was the largest departure from the existing requirements in the name of a better user experience and it was the right decision. Sometimes, you have to recognize that you’ve gone down the wrong path and re-adjust, even if it means you may have to throw out some work.

VERSION 1: full maintains current On Demand structure while bifurcating the new version of the scheduled flow; the scheduled flow asks for details first, then funnels user down hourly or flat; includes redesigned scheduled screens

VERSION 2: gets rid of current entry screen that bifurcates On Demand and schedules; asks "when" first and then divideds into On Demand, hourly or flat; includes redesigned scheduled screens

VERSION 3: starts with OD time and location screen; asks "when and where" first and then divides into OD or scheduled; includes redesigned screens

FINAL FLOW: based of version 3 and optized

A Successful Approach

Designing for the user, not the application

Designing for the user first, application second creates both an intuitive and viable design. With this flow, the first screen asks what is likely to be on the user's mind - when and where do you want to be picked up?

The default inputs are for the current time and current location so that the On Demand user can move forward without ever interacting with the date or address controls. The On Demand flow is kept simple with the user needs in mind. We were designing for the user in a hurry, who needs a car now - so all the inputs are defaulted and the user simply has to click 2 buttons to be matched with a driver.

At the same time, if the user is looking to schedule for the future, they can easily change the pickup location, date and time, by tapping on the fields. If the user selects a time and date that is more than an hour out, they are sent down the “Scheduled” path which asks for a few more inputs than the On Demand path. For the longer “Scheduled” path, the app sets expectations elegantly by using a progress bar on the button as you move through the flow.

Conclusion

On Demand is currently still in development at Limos.com. I left the company shortly after the final flow went into development. I wish the project the most success and can't wait to see the outcome.



The main lesson I took away from this project was never to prioritize shipping something over shipping the right thing. I believe our emphasis on time to market in at the start of the project, really blinded us from seeing that the right thing to do was to create a holistic experience, rather than to tack On Demand onto our existing functionality. Had we taken a holistic approach from the start, we may have saved some time. However, I was lucky to have been working with a team that was so resilient once we realized what the right path was and know the on Demand app will turn out to be a great product.
